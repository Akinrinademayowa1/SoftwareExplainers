Overview

SkuulQuiz PRO is an examination management platform built around one idea: the people setting up a test and the people taking it should each get an interface built for their job, not a compromise between the two.

A school runs on it through three connected roles. A Super Admin owns the school itself — its name, its branding, who else has access. An Admin runs the day-to-day work of a term: building question banks, opening tests, recording scores. A Student only ever sees what they need to: pick a subject, sit the test, see the result.

📝
Two question formats
Multiple choice with 2–8 options, or True/False — mixed freely within the same test.
🖼️
Image-rich questions
Attach up to four images per question. Students tap to enlarge during the test.
⏱️
Per-test timing
Set a duration for each subject and test type independently, or leave it untimed.
📊
Built-in analytics
Ten charts covering performance, timing, and trends — no spreadsheet required.
📤
CSV & PDF exports
Move scores into your own systems, or generate a printable, ranked score sheet.
🔁
Retake workflow
Students can request a second attempt; admins approve or decline from one screen.

What It Solves

Most schools don't lack tools — they have too many of them. A question bank in one document, scores in a spreadsheet, results printed by hand, and no single place that shows the whole picture. SkuulQuiz PRO exists to collapse that into one system that covers a test's entire life, from the first question written to the last chart read.

✕ The scattered way
✕Scores live across spreadsheets, paper registers, and whoever's laptop has the latest version
✕Paper results get re-typed into a gradebook by hand, mistakes and all
✕Seeing a trend means building your own chart from scratch, if anyone gets around to it
✕One shared login — no way to tell who changed a score, or when
✕Retakes get handled by word of mouth, with nothing written down
✕Every result sheet formatted by hand before it can be printed or shared
✓ With SkuulQuiz PRO
✓Every score lives in one place, searchable by student, subject, or term
✓Manual entry and CSV import feed the same system a live test does — nothing re-typed twice
✓Ten charts update automatically the moment a score is recorded
✓Every score carries a source badge and sits in an activity log — an honest record of who did what
✓Retakes go through a request-and-approval flow, visible to everyone involved
✓Branded, ranked PDF score sheets, generated in one tap

Built around how a term actually runs

The design choices below aren't features for their own sake — each one exists because of a specific, ordinary way this kind of system usually breaks.

🧩
One system, start to finish
Question banking, testing, scoring, and analytics stay in one place — nothing to export, re-import, or keep in sync between separate tools.
🪪
Interfaces built per role
A Super Admin, an Admin, and a Student never see the same screen — because they're never trying to do the same job.
🌉
Bridges paper and digital
A test taken on paper and a test taken live end up in the exact same record, with the exact same exports — the system doesn't care which one happened.
🧾
Nothing changes quietly
Source badges and activity logs mean a corrected score is still traceable to what it was corrected from — no silent overwrites.
🗂️
Non-destructive by default
Removing a session tidies a dropdown, not your records. Deleting real data is a deliberate, separate, clearly-labeled action.
📶
Built for real conditions
Images compress automatically on upload, answers auto-save through the test, and a lost connection doesn't cost a student their progress.
🎯
What this page won't do
Claim it's the only option, or the best one for every school. It's built with specific, deliberate opinions about how a term should run — read through the guides below and judge for yourself whether those opinions match how your school actually works.

The Alternatives — and Where They Break

Almost no school starts from nothing. Most already run on some mix of paper, spreadsheets, a generic form or quiz tool, and a lot of coordination over chat. Each of those works, up to a point. Here's specifically where that point tends to be — not as a knock on any of them, but as the actual reasoning behind what got built here instead.

📄
Paper & manual marking
The original system, and still the most common one.
Totaling a score is manual arithmetic, every single time — one mis-add and the result is wrong.
→Objective questions are scored the instant they're marked. There's no manual totaling step left to get wrong.
A lost, damaged, or misplaced answer sheet is a lost result, with nothing to fall back on.
→Once a test is submitted or a score is entered, it exists as a record. There's no physical object left to lose.
Spotting a pattern across a stack of scripts — which question tripped everyone up, which students are slipping — takes manual tallying that rarely happens in practice.
→The same data renders as charts automatically. Nothing needs to be tallied by hand to see it.
Retakes get arranged verbally, with no record of who agreed to what, or when.
→A retake is a logged request with an explicit accept or reject — there's always a record to point back to.
📊
Spreadsheets
Where most digital score-keeping actually lives.
A spreadsheet has no concept of roles — anyone with the file can see, and usually edit, everything in it.
→Every account is scoped by role. A student can never see another student's data; an Admin never reaches Super Admin-only controls.
It's a ledger, not an exam room — it stores a result after the fact, but something else still has to actually administer the test.
→One system runs the test and stores the result. There's nothing separate to reconcile.
Formulas can be edited or broken by anyone with access, and a broken formula often fails silently — a wrong total that looks right until someone checks.
→Question marks and test totals are structural, not formula-dependent. There's no cell to accidentally overwrite.
Two people editing the same file at once risk overwriting each other's changes, usually without any warning that it happened.
→Every edit is scoped to the person who made it and logged individually — simultaneous work doesn't collide invisibly.
There's no built-in trail of who changed a number and when, unless someone separately sets up version history.
→Every score carries a source badge and an entry in the activity log, with no extra setup required.
🧷
Generic online quiz & form builders
Built for one-off surveys and single quizzes, then stretched to cover a full term.
Built around a single one-off quiz or survey, not a recurring, term-long grading structure.
→First Test, Second Test, Third Test, and Exam each carry a fixed, consistent total from the start — the structure is native, not something you configure by hand each time.
Responses usually land in one flat list, not organized by subject, term, or test type.
→Every score is scoped to exactly those three from the moment it's recorded.
Rarely stop the same person submitting the same quiz more than once, unless someone builds that restriction in separately.
→Duplicate submissions are blocked by default — a formal retake request is the only way past it.
A test taken on paper stays completely disconnected from the ones taken digitally — two separate records, two separate places to look.
→Manually entered and digitally submitted scores land in the exact same record, told apart only by a small source badge.
Where image support exists at all, it's rarely built for several images on one question with automatic size handling — large files either aren't supported or slow everything down.
→Up to four images per question, compressed automatically the moment they're uploaded.
💬
Chat apps & word of mouth
How most of the coordination actually happens, tool or no tool.
A retake request becomes a conversation with no timestamp and no way to check back later on who agreed to what.
→It's a logged request with an explicit accept or reject action attached to it.
Sharing a class's results usually means a screenshot or a message typed out by hand, reformatted from scratch every time.
→A branded, ranked, printable score sheet is one tap away.
There's no single place to check "did this actually happen" — it's scattered across whichever conversation it happened in.
→Every action lands in one activity log, in order, and stays searchable.
⚖️
A fair caveat
Broad, general-purpose learning platforms exist too, and plenty of them do combine content, discussion, and assessment in one place. That breadth is exactly the tradeoff — when a tool is built to also be a course library and a forum and a content hub, the testing-specific pieces are usually one feature among many rather than the focus. A tool built only around running tests doesn't have to make that compromise.

None of this means paper, a spreadsheet, or a quick form is the wrong call for a single one-off quiz — they're not. The seams above tend to show up specifically once testing becomes routine: several subjects, several terms, more than one person involved. That's the exact gap this was built to close.

Sessions & Terms

All academic data — students, questions, and scores — is organized under a Session (e.g. an academic year) and a Term within it. This keeps years and terms from bleeding into each other.

You'll always have one active Session and Term selected — it's shown at the top of every screen that depends on it. Switching the active Session/Term instantly switches the students, questions, and scores you're looking at, without deleting anything from the one you switched away from.

💡
Removing a session is non-destructive
Deleting a Session only removes it from your dropdown list. Everything recorded under it — students, tests, scores — stays intact in storage. If you ever re-add a Session with the exact same name, that data reconnects automatically.
// 05 — Core Concepts

Subjects

Subjects are scoped to a Session and Term — add them once per term, and every question, test, and score record hangs off a subject name.

Subject names are automatically standardized when you add them: trimmed of extra spaces and set to uppercase. This exists for one reason — Mathematics, mathematics, and Mathematics   would otherwise be treated as three different subjects by anything that groups or charts by subject name, quietly splitting your data. Standardizing on entry means that never happens.

// 05 — Core Concepts

Grading Scheme

Every subject is assessed across four test types in a term. Each one carries a fixed maximum score, so results are comparable across subjects and across the whole school.

Test typeMaximum scoreTypical use
First Test20Early-term continuous assessment
Second Test20Mid-term continuous assessment
Third Test20Late-term continuous assessment
Exam60End-of-term examination

A student's full-term picture for a subject is the sum of all four: 20 + 20 + 20 + 60 = 100. Question marks within a test are up to whoever builds it — the app doesn't force an even split, only that the test type's total is what appears on score sheets and exports.

Your Account

A Super Admin account is the root of a school on SkuulQuiz PRO. Everything else — Admins, students, subjects, tests — exists underneath one.

Creating an account

  1. From the sign-in screen, switch to the registration tab.
  2. Enter your full name and a valid email address — this becomes how you sign in, so it needs to be one you have access to.
  3. Choose a username and set a password.
  4. Submit. You're signed in immediately, with a fresh school ready to configure.

Signing in

Super Admins sign in with email and password. Use the eye icon on the password field if you want to confirm what you've typed before submitting.

// 06 — Super Admin Guide

School Profile

Your school's identity — name and logo — appears wherever it's relevant: the account menu, the profile page, and on exported score sheets.

  • School name — set from Settings. Used as the header on every PDF score sheet you export.
  • Logo — upload an image and it's automatically resized and optimized for storage before saving. Once set, it also becomes your account avatar throughout the interface — no separate upload needed.
🖼️
Transparent logos stay transparent
If you upload a PNG with a transparent background, the transparency is preserved through the resizing step — it won't be flattened onto a solid color the way some image processors do.
// 06 — Super Admin Guide

Sessions & Terms

Set these up before anything else — most of the app assumes an active Session and Term exist.

  1. Open Settings and find the Session field.
  2. Type a new session name (for example, a year range) and select Add.
  3. Choose a Term from the list next to it.
  4. Save. This pair is now your active context everywhere else in the app.

To remove a session you no longer need in the dropdown, select it and use the delete control beside the list. As covered in Core Concepts, this only tidies the list — the underlying records are kept.

// 06 — Super Admin Guide

Subjects

Add every subject your school teaches for the active Session and Term — Admins will build question banks and record scores against exactly these names.

  1. In Settings, find the Subjects field.
  2. Type a subject name and add it. It's automatically trimmed and capitalized for consistency.
  3. Repeat for each subject, then save.

Subjects can be removed the same way they're added, as long as no question bank or score record still depends on them — remove usage first if you need to retire one mid-term.

// 06 — Super Admin Guide

Admin Accounts

If you're not the only person managing tests, create an Admin (ADM) account for each additional staff member.

  1. Go to the Admins tab (Super Admin only).
  2. Select Add Admin.
  3. Set a username and password for them — they'll also need your school email to sign in, so make sure they have it.
  4. Save. The account is active immediately.

From the same screen you can edit an Admin's password, deactivate their access without deleting the account, or remove them entirely.

🪪
How Admins sign in
An Admin enters their own username, your school email (to identify which school they belong to), and their password. This is different from your own Super Admin sign-in, which uses only email and password.

Dashboard & Analytics

The Analytics tab is the first thing you see after signing in — a read-only summary of every score recorded for the active Session and Term.

Headline numbers

  • Total Attempts — every test submission recorded
  • Unique Students — distinct students who've submitted at least one test
  • Average Score — mean score as a percentage, across all subjects and test types
  • Pass Rate — share of submissions at 50% or above
  • Subjects Covered — how many subjects have at least one recorded score

Charts

Ten charts break the same data down from different angles: a submission timeline, subject-by-subject comparison, score distribution, test type mix, submission hours, top performers, grade distribution, pass/fail split, a monthly trend, and a day-of-week pattern. Together they answer questions a single table can't — like whether a subject's average is drifting term over term, or when students are actually sitting tests.

📊
Empty until there's data
Analytics only has something to show once at least one score exists for the active Session and Term. Record or import a few scores first if the dashboard looks empty.
// 07 — Admin Guide

Managing Students

Students are added per Session and Term, with a login ID, a matric number, a password, and the subjects they're enrolled in.

  1. Open the Students tab and select Add Student.
  2. Enter their name and matric number — this is what they'll type to sign in.
  3. Set an initial password (they can be given this directly; there's no separate reset-by-email flow).
  4. Select which subjects they're enrolled in.
  5. Save.

The student list scrolls within its own panel rather than stretching the page — useful once a class list runs into the hundreds. Each entry can be edited or removed individually.

// 07 — Admin Guide

Creating Questions

Every question belongs to one subject and one test type. Build a full bank before opening that test to students.

Question type

Toggle between Multiple Choice and True/False at the top of the form. Multiple choice starts at 4 options but can be narrowed to 2 or expanded up to 8 using + Add Option and the remove control on each row. True/False fixes the two options for you.

Marking the correct answer

Tap the circle beside the correct option — there's no need to retype the answer text separately, and no risk of a typo breaking the match between what you meant and what gets scored.

Images

Attach up to 4 images per question, 2MB each at upload. Every image is automatically compressed before it's stored, so a full-resolution phone photo doesn't balloon the size of your question bank. During the test, students see a single image inline, or a horizontal gallery if there's more than one — tapping any image opens it full-screen.

Explanation

An optional field shown to the student after they submit, alongside whether they got the question right. Leave it blank for questions that don't need one — it's never required.

✓
Duplicate detection
If you add a question with text that already exists in that test, you'll be asked to confirm before it's added a second time — a quiet safeguard against re-adding the same question after a page refresh.
// 07 — Admin Guide

Test Settings

Duration and instructions are set independently for every subject + test type combination — a 30-minute First Test and an untimed Exam for the same subject can coexist without conflict.

  • Duration — in seconds. Set to 0 for no time limit.
  • Instructions — free text shown to the student before they begin, for anything specific to that test.

These settings save per combination and override any default — changing the Exam's duration never touches the First Test's.

// 07 — Admin Guide

Score Records

Every submitted test lands here automatically, grouped by subject. Scores that didn't come from a live test — a paper exam, a verbal assessment, anything done outside the app — can be added the same way.

Viewing scores

Each subject's records sit in their own scrollable panel with a fixed height and a header that stays pinned as you scroll — so a subject with a thousand records never forces you to scroll past all of them just to reach the next subject.

Manual Score Entry

Select Add Manual Score to open a compact form: pick a registered student (or type details for someone not yet in the roster), subject, test type, score, and total marks. It saves exactly like a real submission — it appears in Analytics, exports, and blocks that student from also taking the test electronically, since a result already exists.

Editing a record

Every row has an edit control that reopens the same form, pre-filled. Every score record also carries a small badge showing where it came from:

🖥️ QuizSubmitted live by a student
📝 ManualEntered by hand for a test taken outside the app
✏️ EditedA quiz submission that's since been corrected
📤 CSVBrought in through the CSV Editor

The badge updates automatically based on history — correcting a live quiz submission relabels it ✏️ Edited rather than hiding that it was ever a real attempt; correcting a manual or CSV record leaves its original label alone.

CSV Editor

A dedicated tab for bulk-editing scores — upload a spreadsheet, correct it in a live grid, and save straight back to score records.

  1. Open the CSV Editor tab and choose the subject and test type you're working with. Total marks fills in automatically based on the test type.
  2. Either upload a CSV file, or select load from existing scores to pull already-saved records into the grid without needing a file at all.
  3. Rows with a problem — a missing ID, a non-numeric or out-of-range score — are flagged immediately.
  4. Tap the pencil on any row to edit it in a focused popup: Student ID, name, and score. Confirm to update the grid.
  5. Use + Add Row for anyone missing from the file, or the ✕ on a row to drop it.
  6. Once every row is valid, select Save to Scores. Existing records for the same student, subject, and test type are updated in place; everyone else is added as new.
StudentID, StudentName, Score

That's the expected column layout — the same shape the CSV export produces, so a file you download, correct in a spreadsheet program, and re-upload will read back in without any reformatting.

A Download CSV button on the grid lets you keep a copy of whatever's currently loaded, corrections included.

Retake Requests

A student who's already submitted a subject and test type can't take it again by default — they'll see an explanation with an option to notify you instead of a second attempt.

Pending requests appear at the top of the Logs tab, badged with a count in the sidebar so you don't need to go looking for them. Each one shows who's asking and for what, with two actions:

  • Accept Retake — clears their existing result for that subject and test type, opening it back up
  • Reject — declines the request; their original result stands

Activity Logs

A running record of what's happened under your school — questions added, scores edited, admins created, data exported. Useful as a paper trail when more than one person has access, or when something needs explaining after the fact.

Signing In

You'll need four things from your school: an Admin ID or school code, your matric number, your password, and which subject and test type you're sitting.

  1. Enter your school's Admin ID (ask your teacher if you don't have it).
  2. Enter your matric number and password.

// 08 — Student Guide

Taking a Test

  • If a question has an image, tap it to view it full-screen — this works for a single image or a gallery of several.
  • Your answers save automatically every 30 seconds, so a lost connection or an accidental refresh mid-test doesn't cost you your progress.
  • If the test is timed, the countdown is visible the entire time — submit before it reaches zero.
  • Review your answers using the question navigator before selecting Submit — once submitted, the test is locked.
// 08 — Student Guide

Results & Retakes

Right after submitting, you'll see your score and a full breakdown — which questions you got right, which you missed, and the correct answer for each. Where the admin has added one, an explanation appears alongside the answer.

Leaving this screen — closing the tab, refreshing, navigating away — takes you back to sign-in rather than re-opening the test. Your result is already recorded at that point, so there's nothing to lose by leaving.

If you believe your result doesn't reflect what happened — a technical issue, a genuine second attempt needed — use the option on screen to notify your admin. They'll see your request and can reopen the test if they choose to.

FAQ

Can a student take the same test twice?

Not on their own — a second attempt requires an admin to accept a retake request first. This prevents accidental duplicate submissions from inflating or overwriting a real result.

What happens if I forget my password?

Students and Admins have their password reset by whoever manages their account — an Admin can reset a student's password from the Students tab, and a Super Admin can reset an Admin's from the Admins tab. Super Admins should keep their own email and password somewhere safe, since that's the only way in.

Can two schools use the same subject names?

Yes — every school's data is fully separate. Subjects, students, and scores never cross between schools, even if the names match exactly.

Does switching to dark mode change anything besides the color scheme?

No — it's purely visual. All data, scores, and settings behave identically in either mode.

Can I change a test's total marks after questions already exist?

The total is the sum of each question's marks, so adjust it by editing individual question marks rather than a single field — there's no single override that would leave question-level marks inconsistent with the displayed total.

Is there a limit to how many students or questions I can add?

Practical limits exist per test (up to 200 questions) and per question (up to 8 options, 4 images) to keep things fast and usable — there's no hard cap on the number of students.
